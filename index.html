About

“History develops, art stands still.”


E. M. Forster

Many people are giving and gave their lives to study and make art, sometimes becoming someone famous, sometimes keeping to the shadows with the humble purpose of enriching the human knowledge about our past and cultural expressions. In many cases, will be the institutions to recognize their efforts, giving them a space among their collections. But, why them? Why those and not other institutions preserve their collection and funds?

Geohistorians proposes an analysis on art historians and the keepers that preserve their collections. Our data sample is composed by the Italian art historians recorded in Dictionary of Art Historians and ArtChives.
The Dictionary of Art Historians is a free, privately funded biographical dictionary of historians of western art written and maintained by scholars, while ArtChives collects detailed archival descriptions of notable art historians' collections.
Finally, we integrated the extracted information through Wikidata, one of the most famous collaborative and open source databases.

The workflow

The project workflow was articulated in four different phases: the data cleaning and access, for which some data have been integrated automatically and manually, enriching the original database; the data analysis, where some patterns were found and exposed in the data visualization process, finally integrated and commented in the storytelling and web development phase. In the last step, we could make some assumptions limited to our database results.
You can find the cleaning and analysis phases on our Jupyter Notebook.

Conclusioni

Research Question #1: What is the distribution in the Italian territory of the keepers?
Conclusion: Many charts belonging to the "Database Overview" group of queries provide us with an answer to the first research question. Indeed, our dataset seems to give keepers and cities from the North of Italy a position of predominance as they are more described in detail by the number of historians they own. Even the historians born or died in the North of Italy are much more represented than the historians coming from the South. From one hand, it could be legible that cities with a large tradition of artists and historians such as Rome and Florence have a predominant position, but on the other hand it cannot be denied that also cities like Naples and Palermo were important cultural spot in the past. Thus, it is really unjustified that these cities are so under represented in our dataset. Furthermore, this is a huge problem for contemporary historians of art history who want to focus on South of Italy as our dataset is not that helpful for them. In addition to that, we can notice that besides big institutions like Scuola Normale Superiore and Fondazione Federico Zero the most of the keepers are small and conserve only one art historian. As a result, in Italy the conservation of the cultural heritage left by art historian is very fragmented in the territory. To conclude, the overral view of our dataset testifies how the Italian keepers are many and disseminated in the territory with few and all North-baser important cities such as Florence, Rome and Bologna.
Research Question #2: What is the role and the consequences of the digital technologies with respect to the Italian archival world?
Conclusion: Especially the charts belonging to the "Historians and Keepers" group of queries are useful to answer to this research question.  Indeed, it seems like in Italy there is only a single digital libraries in charge of conserving art historians: BEIC Digital Library, based in Milan. It's really interesting that this single Italian digital library conserves the highest number of historians (10) and that Milan is one of the cities that own more historians even though many of the conserved historians didn't die neither were born there. This is a singularity in our dataset  and it is thanks to the BEIC Digital Library which conserves up to 10 historians. These data lead us to understand the differences between the digital and the physical paradigm. Where there is no digital keeper, the historians' collection are likely conserved in the same physical place where the historian either died or was born so that the physical locarion plays a crucial role in deciding which keeper will own the collection. This could also be the reason for which many smaller cities in Italy own some keepers: that is, since the historians either were born or died in those smaller cities their collection stayed where they were left. The digital paradigm, instead, works differently so as to break the physical limits. Indeed, it does not matter where the historian died or was born because their collection will be conserved by digital keepers which can have physical headquarters elsewhere. Given that, we can conclude that the Italian archival world is more rooted to the physical world, as the data prove.
Research Question #3: Is there a correlation between the kind of the keepers and the historians they conserve?
Conclusion: Even though further analysis should be done in order to have a sharped comprehension, the last charts seem to suggest that there could be a correlation between the kind of keeper and the historian conserved. Indeed, for instance the libraries seem to prefer historians born in their places, whereas archives seem to prefer historians who died in their places.A possible reason for that can be found in the nature of the keeper itself. It is also worth noticing how the most frequent keepers for historian neither died or born in their places are less conventional types of keepers such as open-access publisher or website. However, as we have said, the data are not enough to perform a sharper analysis and we cannot make any further statement about this topic.
